What is Islamic Inheritance?

Islamic inheritance, also known as 'Mirath' or 'Fara'id', is the distribution of a deceased Muslim's property and assets to their heirs in accordance with the principles of Islamic law. The rules of inheritance are specified in the Quran and are designed to ensure a just and equitable distribution of wealth among family members.

This calculator provides a simplified calculation based on the most common scenarios. For complex cases, it is highly recommended to consult a qualified Islamic scholar.

Distribution of Shares

Key Heirs and Their Shares

The Quran specifies fixed shares for certain primary heirs. Here are some of the key heirs and their prescribed shares:

  • Husband: Receives 1/2 if the deceased has no children, and 1/4 if she has children.
  • Wife: Receives 1/4 if the deceased has no children, and 1/8 if he has children.
  • Son: Receives a double share compared to a daughter.
  • Daughter: A single daughter receives 1/2. Two or more daughters collectively receive 2/3.
  • Father: Receives 1/6 if the deceased has children.
  • Mother: Receives 1/6 if the deceased has children, and 1/3 if there are no children.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the importance of a Will (Wasiyyah) in Islam?

A Will, or Wasiyyah, is a legal document that allows a Muslim to bequeath up to one-third of their wealth to non-heirs. The remaining two-thirds of the estate must be distributed among the prescribed heirs according to Islamic law. A Will is highly recommended in Islam to ensure that one's wishes are fulfilled after death.

Do other relatives receive a share of the inheritance?

Yes, besides the primary heirs, other relatives such as grandparents, grandchildren, and siblings may also be entitled to a share of the inheritance, depending on the specific circumstances of the deceased's family.
