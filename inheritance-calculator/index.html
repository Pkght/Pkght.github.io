    Understanding Islamic Inheritance

    Islamic inheritance, known as Mirath or Faraid, is a structured system of wealth distribution defined in the Quran and Sunnah. It ensures fairness, prevents disputes, and protects the financial rights of family members after someone passes away.

    Primary Heirs & Their Quranic Shares

    Islamic inheritance laws assign specific shares to certain family members. Below are the most common examples based on the Quranic verses (Surah An-Nisa):

    • Husband: 1/2 share if there are no children, 1/4 if there are children.
    • Wife: 1/4 share if no children, 1/8 if the deceased has children.
    • Son: Receives twice the share of a daughter.
    • Daughter: A single daughter receives 1/2; multiple daughters share 2/3.
    • Father: Receives 1/6 if there are children.
    • Mother: Receives 1/6 if there are children; 1/3 if no children exist.

    Additional relatives may also inherit depending on specific circumstances. Since inheritance can become detailed, this tool simplifies the most frequent cases.

    Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

    What is a Wasiyyah (Islamic Will)?

    A Wasiyyah allows a Muslim to allocate up to one-third of their wealth to non-heirs. The remaining two-thirds must go to the Quranic heirs. Making a Will is highly encouraged in Islam.

    Do siblings inherit?

    Yes, siblings may inherit if certain primary heirs are not present. This calculator focuses on mainstream cases, so for detailed scenarios, it's recommended to seek scholarly guidance.

    Is this calculator 100% accurate?

    It follows commonly accepted Islamic rules for standard inheritance situations. However, many cases involve unique conditions. Always confirm final distribution with a qualified Islamic scholar.
